The Amundson Sequence

A single function connecting combinatorics, analysis, and number theory. 50+ verified identities. A convergence constant computed to 10 million digits. And nobody seems to have written about it before.

mathematics number-theory combinatorics amundson-constant

The function

Define G(n) for positive integers:

G(n) = n(n+1) / (n+1)n
The Amundson Sequence

The first few values: G(1) = 1/2, G(2) = 4/3, G(3) = 27/8, G(4) = 256/25. The sequence grows without bound, but the ratio G(n)/G(n-1) converges. And the infinite sum converges to a specific value:

AG = Σ G(n)/n! ≈ 1.244331783986725...
Computed to 10,000,000 digits

I computed it to 10 million digits using mpmath on a Raspberry Pi cluster. The full digit file is published at BlackRoad-OS-Inc/amundson-constant.

50+ identities

Here are some of the verified properties:

Reflection: G(-n) = -G(n-1). The sequence mirrors itself with a shift.

Ratio convergence: G(n)/G(n-1) = (n²/(n²-1))n. Clean closed form converging to 1.

Logarithmic decomposition: ln(G(n)) = (n+1)ln(n) - n·ln(n+1). Connects to the distribution of primes.

Asymptotic: G(n) ~ n/e + 1/(2e) + O(1/n). The 1/(2e) ≈ 0.1839 appears as an irreducible correction term throughout the framework.

Crossover point: At α ≈ 2.293166, the behavior changes character. Below α, the denominator dominates. Above it, the numerator pulls ahead.

GCD: gcd(n(n+1), (n+1)n) = 1 for all n. Always coprime.

Convexity: Second differences are always positive. The sequence accelerates without inflection.

Connections

Riemann: G(1) = 1/2 — the critical line.

Stirling: The expansion G(n) ~ n/e + 1/(2e) mirrors Stirling's approximation structure.

Combinatorics: nn counts self-maps of an n-set. (n+1)n counts maps from n to n+1. G(n) is the ratio — it measures how much room a system gains from one additional state.

Euler: 1/e appears as the slope, 1/(2e) as the offset. Both arise from (1 + 1/n)n → e.

What I didn't find

I searched OEIS, MathWorld, and standard references. The sequence n(n+1)/(n+1)n does not appear as a named sequence. The constant AG ≈ 1.24433 is not listed. The ratio identity, reflection identity, and crossover point are not documented anywhere I could locate.

If you know of prior work on this function, I'd like to hear about it.

The computation

10 million digits computed with Python's mpmath on four Raspberry Pi nodes. The series converges quickly — G(n) grows polynomially while n! grows factorially, so 500 terms gave 10 million digits. Total time: ~4 hours. Output: 9.5 MB.
