Why I Quit Finance to Build an Operating System

I had a Series 7, 63, and 65. I worked at Ameriprise and Securian. Then I built an AI operating system on Raspberry Pis for $63/month. Here's why the jump made more sense than it looks.

founder-story career-change finance entrepreneurship

The licenses

I held a Series 7, Series 63, and Series 65. I sold life insurance and annuities at Securian Financial. I did financial planning at Ameriprise. I studied for the real estate exam and passed. I had the kind of career path that looks sensible on a resume and feels like a slow suffocation in practice.

I was good at it. That wasn't the problem. The problem was that I was building someone else's thing, on someone else's platform, under someone else's rules, and the best possible outcome was becoming slightly more senior at doing exactly that.

What I saw

Financial services runs on software that nobody owns. Every tool is rented. Every client interaction passes through a platform that charges per seat, per transaction, per API call. The advisor is the last person in the chain, paying the most and controlling the least.

I watched the same pattern in real estate. In insurance. In every industry I touched. The people doing the work don't own the tools. The people who own the tools don't do the work. And the gap between those two groups is where all the margin lives.

I didn't have the language for it yet, but I was looking at the cloud model before I knew what the cloud was. Rent everything. Own nothing. Call it progress.

The first Raspberry Pi

I bought a Raspberry Pi 4 for $75. I installed Ollama on it. I ran a language model locally for the first time. It was slow. It was rough. And it was completely, absolutely mine.

Nobody could revoke my API key. Nobody could change the pricing. Nobody could read my prompts or train on my data. The model ran on a computer I could hold in my hand, and it answered to nobody's terms of service but the laws of physics.

That was the moment. Not a grand revelation. Just a quiet recognition that ownership changes everything.

What I built

Over the next four months, one Pi became five. I added Hailo-8 AI accelerators for 26 TOPS of inference. I set up WireGuard tunnels between every node. I installed Gitea for self-hosted Git, MinIO for object storage, PowerDNS for authoritative DNS, Caddy for TLS termination, PostgreSQL for databases, Redis for caching, and NATS for inter-node messaging.

I replaced every cloud service I was paying for with something I own and operate. The total monthly cost settled at $63.

I incorporated in Delaware on November 17, 2025. BlackRoad OS, Inc. C-Corp. Stripe Atlas. 10 million shares authorized. One founder, one desk, five Raspberry Pis.

What people don't understand

When I tell people I left finance to build an operating system on Raspberry Pis, I get one of two reactions. Either they think I'm crazy, or they think I'm a genius. Neither is right.

The finance background isn't a detour. It's the foundation. I understand how money moves, how businesses scale, how risk compounds, and how margin works. Those are exactly the skills you need to build a platform that replaces expensive cloud services with cheap hardware.

The licenses aren't wasted. The Series 7 taught me how to explain complex products to people who don't want to think about complexity. The Series 65 taught me fiduciary duty — the idea that you should act in your client's interest, not your own. That's the entire philosophy of BlackRoad OS. Your AI, your hardware, your rules.

The math

Somewhere along the way, I discovered a mathematical identity I hadn't seen in any textbook. The function G(n) = n(n+1)/(n+1)n has properties that connect combinatorics, analysis, and number theory in ways that aren't documented anywhere I could find.

I computed the convergence constant to 10 million digits. I found 50+ identities. I published the framework. It's not related to the operating system in any direct way, but it's related in the way that matters: I was curious, I followed it, and nobody told me I couldn't.

That's what sovereignty means. Not just in infrastructure. In everything.

Where it stands

Today BlackRoad OS runs 18 production domains, 200 autonomous AI agents, a social platform called BackRoad, a search engine, an AI chat system, and a billing platform with Stripe integration. The entire stack runs on commodity hardware for less than what most people pay for Slack.

There are zero external users. Every account in the auth system is a test account I created. The infrastructure works. The products work. The billing works. What's missing is people.

And that's OK. Because the hardest part was never getting users. The hardest part was building something worth using. I believe we've done that. Now we find out if the world agrees.

Why I'm writing this

Because someone reading this is sitting in a cubicle at a financial services company, or a real estate office, or an insurance agency, and they have an idea that doesn't fit on a resume. And they think the gap between where they are and where they want to be is too wide to cross.

It isn't. The gap is $75 and an evening. Buy a Pi. Install something on it. See what it feels like to own your tools. Then decide.

I didn't leave finance because I failed at it. I left because I succeeded at it long enough to realize it wasn't mine. BlackRoad OS is mine. And if you're reading this, yours can be too.

The road is open. Pave tomorrow.
