Why I Quit Finance to Build an Operating System

I had a Series 7, 63, and 65. I worked at Ameriprise and Securian. Then I built an AI operating system on Raspberry Pis for $38/month. Here's why the jump made more sense than it looks.

The Training Nobody Talks About

People hear "finance to tech" and assume I'm a career switcher who learned to code on the side. That's not what happened.

At Ameriprise, I held the Series 7, 63, and 65. I managed client portfolios. I sat through compliance reviews, risk assessments, and suitability analyses. I learned how capital structures work — not in theory, but in practice. Which assets back which liabilities. How reserve ratios protect institutions from tail risk. How a single point of failure in a dependency chain can take down an entire book of business.

At Securian, I worked with annuities — products that are literally promises about the future backed by present-day reserves. The entire business model is: hold enough in reserve that you can deliver on every commitment, no matter what the market does.

That's not a finance lesson. That's an infrastructure lesson.

The Moment It Clicked

I was using five different cloud services to run a simple project. GitHub for code. Cloudflare for DNS. OpenAI for inference. Slack for communication. Vercel for hosting. Each one had its own login, its own pricing page, its own terms of service, its own way of holding my data hostage.

I realized I was doing the exact thing I'd spent years warning clients against: concentrating risk in counterparties I couldn't control.

In finance, we call this counterparty risk. If your broker goes down, your trades don't execute. If your custodian fails, your assets are frozen. The solution is always the same: own the infrastructure. Hold the reserve. Don't depend on a single point of failure.

So I applied the same logic to software.

$38/Month

Today, BlackRoad OS runs on 5 Raspberry Pis and 2 cloud servers. Total monthly cost: $38.

It replaces GitHub (Gitea, 629 repos), OpenAI (Ollama + Hailo-8, 26 TOPS), Cloudflare (Caddy + PowerDNS), Slack (RoundTrip, 200 AI agents), S3 (MinIO), and Tailscale (WireGuard mesh). Every component is self-hosted. Every byte of data stays on hardware I own.

The reserve ratio concept from insurance maps directly: 90% of BlackRoad's equity is reserved to the corporation. The same way an insurance company holds surplus to meet future claims, BlackRoad holds equity to fund future development without external dilution.

What Finance Actually Teaches You

People think finance is about spreadsheets and stock picks. It's not. It's about understanding what's underneath.

When I look at a cloud service, I don't see a product. I see a capital structure. Who owns the servers? What happens if they raise prices? What's the switching cost? Where does my data go if they shut down? These are the same questions I asked about every investment I recommended to clients.

The answer, almost always, is: you don't know, and you can't find out. That's unacceptable in finance. It should be unacceptable in software too.

The Math Nobody Expected

Along the way, I discovered something unexpected. While studying the function G(n) = n^(n+1)/(n+1)^n — the flip side of the classic limit that gives Euler's number — I found a new mathematical constant. It connects to the Riemann hypothesis, quantum mechanics, and DNA base counting. I computed it to 10 million digits.

That wasn't the plan. But when you build your own tools and run your own compute, you have the freedom to chase whatever catches your attention. No usage limits. No API rate caps. No monthly invoice that scales with curiosity.

Zero Users

I'll be honest: BlackRoad OS has zero external users right now. Every account in the auth system is a test account. The infrastructure is real, the products work, but nobody outside of me has used them yet.

That's okay. The point of this post isn't to sell you something. It's to explain why a person with a finance background would build an operating system, and why the answer is simpler than you'd think: because the skills transfer perfectly.

Risk management is system design. Reserve ratios are resource allocation. Counterparty risk is vendor lock-in. Capital structure is architecture.

The vocabulary changes. The thinking doesn't.
